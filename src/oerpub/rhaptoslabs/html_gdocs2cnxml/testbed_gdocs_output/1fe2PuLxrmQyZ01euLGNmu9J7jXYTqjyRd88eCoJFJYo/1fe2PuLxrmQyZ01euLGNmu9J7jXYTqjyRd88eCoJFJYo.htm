ENERGY IN FOOD

The much of the food that we gain from food is stored in fruits and vegetables in the form of glucose and starch. Fruit seeds and roots can be tested with chemicals to find out if they contain glucose and starch.[c]

Draw pictures to show how to do these tests

                          Test for Starch

  1. Take some food(e.g potato and cut it open on a dish)
  2. Put a few drops of iodine solution on the food.
  3. If the colour of iodine changes from yellow to black then STARCH is present in the food.

                         Test for Glucose

  1. Take some food (e.g banana and cut it into small pieces.
  2. Put it into a test tube and half fill the tube with BENEDICT’S solution.
  3. Heat the tube in a beaker of boiling water.
  4. If the Benedict’s solution changes colour from blue to red then GLUCOSE is present in food.

Renewable resources are the resources that can be used again such as water, wind, solar, biomass and geothermal. Whereas non- renewable resources are those that cannot be used again such as natural gas, nuclear energy,coal and oil. Coal, oil and gas are called fossil fuels because they have been formed from the organic remains of prehistoric plants and animals.

Coal, oil and gas are fossil fuels. They have been made from plants and animals that lived on earth about 100 million years ago. Solar panels can be fitted to houses but this source of renewable energy  is only  available on sunny days.Wind energy can be collected through a windmill or wind -turbine which can be big and noisy. Electricity’s quest for the ground

We now realise that we use electricity for many important things every day

BUT we also need to know that electricty always wants to get to the ground!

Sometimes it may try to go through you to get to the ground.

This will give you an electric shock.

A large electric shock can hurt you badly

It can even kill you, so it is important that we know how to use electricity safely.

10 Safety tips for electricity

1. ALWAYS turn off appliances and the wall switch before taking out or putting in plugs. Never pull out or put in a plug to a socket while the switch is still on. You could get a shock or blow the plug.

4. Never use an appliance that has a cord that is broken or has some of the metal wire showing.

2.Never use electrical appliances outdoors in wet weather or if you are wet. Electricity is attracted to water, so you WILL get a shock if you are touching an applicance and water drips into the socket, cord or motor.

8. Ask an adult to check what you are about to do is safe, before using an electrical appliance.

3. Electricity can flow from one person to the next. NEVER try to pull someone who is being shocked away from the appliance. you will get shocked too! Use a non plastic/non metal object to  separate them from the electrical source.

5. Never play near to or on electric fences or power lines. These both have live electricty running through it so you do not need to be touching it to get a shock!

6. Never put  lots of applicances into one socket. Too much electricty flowing to one plug is dangerous. One multiplug adapter is safe, but do not put adapters into each other. Rather use 2 different plug points.

7. Do not play outside when there is thunder and lightening.  If this powerful natural electricity strikes close to you, it will try to get to the ground through you!

8. Never use electrical appliances in the bathroom. Remember, electricity flows through water.

9. Wear shoes when using electrical appliances. Never use electrical appliances barefoot.

10...Turn off all switches after a power failure. When the power is turned on again, the sudden flow of electricity to all your appliances could cause them to blow.

Unit 2.8: Energy Systems

What is a system?

Key concepts:

  1. When things work together for a specific reason, is called a system.
  2. In nature, systems are found all around us.
  3. People also invent systems all the time, to help them to accomplish something.

Energy Systems

  1. When ENERGY is being released or stored from one step to the next, this system is called an ENERGY system

Bending and stretching

Things that can be bent or stretched, then released, show energy that is stored then released.

Rubber bands are excellent examples of how energy is stored and released by stretching[ai].

Activity

3. After the competition, choose the correct answer:

A. The winner was the person who managed to shoot their band the furthest.

B. The winner was the person who managed to release the most energy from their band

C The winner was the person who managed to store the most energy by stretching, before shooting it.

D All of the above

ANSWER - D all of the above

Falling

Things that fall show energy that is stored then released.[aj]

The water in a dam is another example of energy that is stored then released.

When the flood gates are opened, the water in the dam moves through the gates and falls down to the river below.

The water helps to show us how the energy is at first stored in the dam, then as the water falls, we can see that the energy is released.

Setting [au]the scenario

Adam, Lerato, Yumnah and I love playing with toy cars. We enjoy running through the yard with them. Last year, Adam was in a bad accident which left him paralysed from the waist down. He still loves watching us play, and gets really excited when I choose one of his cars to race across the stoep. If his car beats the others’, it just makes his day! I feel proud to use one of his cars, as he is a good friend of mine, and I know how much it means to him, but its not the same as him playing with us. I am sure there must be a way we can include Adam in playing with cars with us again. What could we make so we can once again all play with cars together?

3. Constraints

Use a selection of these materials:

Car construction: any recyclable materials

Sticking: masking tape, clear glue, Pritt or similar

Axles: skewer sticks, wooden dowls, lollipop sticks or similar

External axle holders: 2 clothes pegs for each holder

Wheels: polystyrene, jar caps, cotton reels, bottle tops or similar

Stoppers and Spacers: small pieces of rubber, plastic, putty, plasticine, prestik, elastic bands or similar

4. Dreaming and Planning to make the vehicle

With the help of your teacher, dream up what your vehicle is going to look like!

Teachers’ Guide thoughts -

Once kids have a picture in their head of what their car looks like, it makes it easier for some of them to plan.

After explaining the components the vehicle must include, and the examples of materials they can use, walk your class through a mental building exercise - allow them build up their car in their head, by your step-by-step mediation, before writing anything down.

1. each child to close their eyes, or put head on desk or have a pen and  scrap paper in hand - depending on how best it helps them to visualise things

2. Once again walk the children through each component of their car, and give them options as you introduce each component and possible materials they could use, allowing them to make choices in their head of what they wish to use for their car.

Sentence starters like the following can assist you in this process:

*First, lets get an idea of the type of car you want to make - do you want a sedan or hatchback car [explain these words]? bus or minibus? tractor? bulldozer? open top?      

- remember if you realise there is not a material that will make the shape you want, it is ok if this changes as we go through this mental building exercise

*Next, lets think about the body of our car - what is it going to be made of - an ice cream container, juice box, flora margarine container, a rounded coke bottle-type shape or anything else you can think of that would make an interesting body.

*Then, lets add some wheels to this body. Think about what type car you have chosen. Imagine what the wheels would look like. Now think about the size of the material you have chosen for your body. What wheels will be best - bottle tops, jar lids, polystyrene circles etc?

*The grip for your wheels is important too - depending on the use of your vehicle, how will your vehicle stick to the road? - sandpaper, matchsticks, rubber? ...or anything else you can think of that would make an interesting wheel with grip.

* What are you going to use for axles? - dowls, skewer sticks, sucker loillipop sticks?

* What are you going to use as spacers to separate the wheel from the body of your vehicle? - cork? small pieces of rubber? plastic? putty? plasticine? prestik? elastic bands? 

*what are you going to use to prevent your wheels from falling off the end of the axle? - cork? small pieces of rubber? plastic? putty? plasticine? prestik? elastic bands?

*Finally, how are you going to add your own touch to this? - What colour is your vehicle going to be? How will you colour it - paint, paper covering, colour it in? Are you going to have any window? a number plate? lights? a tow bar? a bumper? - what will you make these from?

NB: to each question ALWAYS include “...or anything else you can think of that would make an interesting..[component]”

The potential danger of doing this mental exercise is that it could, and does for some children, stiffle creativity of those who are able to imagine their car without any mediation, by putting ideas of materials in their head. However, for those who begin designing without a mental picture  and hence a peek over at their partner’s ideas, this helps them to visualise where they are going.

Children who think best by writing down or drawing, could jot down ideas or shapes while being mediated through the “dreaming” process, instead of just closing their eyes.
